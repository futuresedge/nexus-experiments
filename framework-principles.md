# The Framework and its Principles

The framework is a way to organise humans and AI so they do important work together in a way that is safe, reliable, and keeps getting better over time. 

## What the framework is for

- AI is used to **empower** people, not to replace them at any cost; if something is technically correct but hurts people, concentrates power, or reduces human choice, that is treated as failure, not success. 
- Humans and AI are set up as partners: AI handles repeatable, at-scale tasks, while humans handle judgment, ethics, and one-way decisions that are hard to reverse. 
- The rules that stop harmful behaviour are built into the structure of the system, so some bad actions are simply not possible, instead of being “allowed but against policy”. 

### The two big promises

- Trustworthy process:  
  - Every step is recorded.  
  - Every result is checked against a clear “definition of done”.  
  - No one is asked to “just trust” the person or AI that did the work. 

- Excellent execution:  
  - Problems are understood from different viewpoints before solutions are chosen.  
  - Assumptions are tested with small experiments before big rollouts.  
  - Efficiency is judged across the whole journey from idea to real-world use, not just “how fast did we code this?”. 

### How trust is created

- No result is accepted just because the author says it is right; a separate person or agent checks it against rules that were written before the work started. 
- The main output of any task is structured proof that it meets its agreed goal, not just a nice-looking document or feature. 
- What any actor can do depends on the tools and permissions they actually hold, not their job title or claims. 
- Every action leaves an unchangeable record of who did what, when, and with which tools. 
- The one who does the work cannot be the one who signs it off; and checking can be shared across many verifiers so there is no single point of failure. 

### How performance works

- Tough problems always get multiple perspectives before a solution is picked, often by having different agents or people play clearly different thinking roles. 
- Big or risky work is not scaled up until its key assumptions have been tested through quick, low-cost prototypes. 
- The framework tracks rework, token use, time, and energy across the full lifecycle, and treats high cost or waste as a signal that something in the design or patterns needs to change. 

### How learning is built in

- Whenever an actor hits something they cannot safely decide, they must surface that uncertainty immediately; raising doubts is treated as correct behaviour, not as failure. 
- Any decision that is hard or costly to undo requires a present human to make a deliberate call, with enough context to understand the risks. 
- Every finished task adds structured lessons into a shared Pattern Library that future humans and agents can draw on, so the organisation develops a durable “memory” of what works, what fails, and why. 
- Each actor only sees the minimum context needed for their task, but all of them contribute back to a shared knowledge base, so context stays light while knowledge keeps growing. 
- No work begins until the problem is clearly understood from multiple angles, and “done” is written down and agreed by someone other than the person or agent who will do the work. 

## The principles

### Purpose principles

- P1 — AI that **empowers** people  
  The whole system exists to make people’s lives better, not just to make work faster or cheaper.  
    
- P2 — Humans and AI as partners  
  Humans and AI are set up to work together on purpose: AI does the repeatable, at-scale tasks; humans handle judgment, ethics, and big one-way decisions.  
    
- P3 — Safety built into the system  
  The rules that stop harmful behaviour are built into how the system works, so bad actions are blocked by design, not just by policies or promises.

### Trust principles

- T1 — Checked, not just claimed  
  No result is accepted just because its author says it is correct; someone else must check it against clear criteria agreed in advance.  
    
- T2 — Proof over pretty outputs  
  The main thing any piece of work must produce is solid, well-organised evidence that it actually meets its agreed goal, not just a polished-looking output.  
    
- T3 — Power comes from tools held  
  What any person or agent is allowed to do depends on the tools and permissions they actually hold in the system, not on what they claim or what title they have.  
    
- T4 — Everything is on record  
  Every action is recorded with who did it and when, in a log that cannot be edited or hidden later.  
    
- T5 — No one marks their own work  
  The person or agent that produces something cannot be the one that signs it off as correct; maker and checker must always be separate.  
    
- T6 — Trustworthy even if some fail  
  The system is designed so that outputs stay reliable even if some people or agents make mistakes or act dishonestly.  
    
- T7 — Many can verify, not just one  
  Checking work does not have to be done by one central boss or committee; it can be spread across multiple independent checkers.

### Performance principles

- PF1 — Many viewpoints on hard problems  
  Hard problems are looked at from several different viewpoints before a solution is chosen, instead of relying on a single way of thinking.  
    
- PF2 — Test small before going big  
  You do not roll out big or expensive work until you have tested the key assumptions with small, low-cost experiments or prototypes.  
    
- PF3 — Efficient from idea to reality  
  You judge efficiency over the whole journey from “what is the problem?” to “this is running in the real world”, not by how fast any single step is.  
    
- PF4 — Spend more on what matters  
  Spending of tokens, compute, and energy is treated as a design choice and a signal: the system should spend more on work that matters more, and notice when work is wasteful.

### Learning principles

- L1 — Speak up as soon as unsure  
  Whenever an actor hits something they cannot properly handle, they must raise it right away, without worrying if it seems “big enough” or whether they’ll be blamed.  
    
- L2 — Humans decide on one-way moves  
  Decisions that can’t be undone must always involve a present, clearly responsible human making a conscious call.  
    
- L3 — Every task teaches the system  
  Every finished piece of work adds clear, structured lessons to a shared library, so the organisation gets steadily smarter over time.  
    
- L4 — Just enough info, shared wisdom  
  Each actor only sees the information they need to do their current task well, while the deeper knowledge and patterns are stored in a shared place for everyone.  
    
- L5 — Understand the problem first  
  No one starts designing solutions until the problem has been understood from multiple viewpoints, with real evidence and attention to the people affected.  
    
- L6 — Define “done” before starting  
  Work does not start until “what done means” is written down, agreed, and defined by someone other than the person or agent who will do the work.
